01 — The problem

A high-volume payout that nobody can fully check by hand.

Manufacturers sell to wholesalers at the Wholesale Acquisition Cost (WAC, the list price). But hospitals, clinics, GPOs and 340B entities hold contracts entitling them to lower prices. When a wholesaler sells at the contracted price, it has been paid less than the WAC it paid the manufacturer — and bills the difference back. That credit is the chargeback.

Chargebacks are among the largest gross-to-net deductions a manufacturer carries. The volume runs to millions of lines, the contracts are complex, and validation is manual. A meaningful share of what gets paid is duplicated, mispriced, or claimed against the wrong contract — direct, recoverable leakage.

Shanta Pharma Limited wants to size that leakage and build an analytics capability that flags invalid claims before they are paid — without disrupting legitimate distributor relationships.

02 — The dataset

One labelled ledger, three master tables.

The chargeback economics are real: chargeback = (WAC − contract price) × quantity. Drug codes and per-unit acquisition costs are drawn from the public CMS NADAC file; contracts, customers, transactions and error labels are simulated, and every line carries a ground-truth label so models can be trained and scored.

The two-table design — ledger versus contract master — is deliberate. It forces a reconciliation step against a source of truth, which is the actual day-job of chargeback operations, not a single-CSV anomaly toy.

Honest note on the data

This is an academic exercise. “Shanta Pharma Limited” is an illustrative manufacturer, not a real company’s data. The dataset is synthetic: drug codes and acquisition costs come from the public CMS NADAC file, but WAC markups, contract terms, error rates and the error mix are modelling assumptions chosen to be plausible — not measured industry figures. The ~12% value-at-risk is a property of the simulation and should be read as illustrative. The generator is included so every number is reproducible.
